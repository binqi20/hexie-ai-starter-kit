# SHR Example: De-Identified HR Policy or Work-Design Analysis

Audience: Strategic Human Resources Management students.

## Example question

How can an organization revise a hybrid-work policy to improve fairness, productivity, and employee wellbeing?

## Safe source pack

Use:

- Public HR policy examples.
- De-identified course cases.
- Public survey summaries.
- Academic articles on work design, hybrid work, or employee wellbeing.

Do not upload:

- Employee names.
- Payroll data.
- Performance records.
- Disciplinary records.
- Internal HR files without authorization.
- Free-text survey comments that can identify individuals.

## Starter source manifest

| ID | Source | Source status | Why included | Human check |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SHR-S1 | Work design article | opened_full_text | Evidence on autonomy and wellbeing | Check construct definitions |
| SHR-S2 | Public hybrid-work policy | opened_full_text | Policy language example | Check organization context |
| SHR-S3 | De-identified survey table | opened_full_text | Practice dataset | Check de-identification |

## Claim-evidence examples

| Claim | Evidence needed | Support status |
|---|---|---|
| Hybrid work affects employees differently depending on role autonomy. | Article or dataset anchor. | NOT_CHECKED |
| One policy rule will be fair for all departments. | Likely unsupported; needs context-specific evidence. | NOT_CHECKED |
| Employee comments reveal identifiable health or family information. | Treat as sensitive; do not upload. | NOT_CHECKED |

## Human gate

Check:

- Whether the data is truly de-identified.
- Whether a policy recommendation affects real employees.
- Whether fairness claims consider role, department, gender, care responsibility, and power differences.
- Whether correlation is being mistaken for causation.

## Suggested output

A structured policy review:

- Policy goal.
- Stakeholder groups.
- Evidence-supported risks.
- Options.
- Trade-offs.
- Implementation checks.
- What HR must decide, not AI.
